# Solar Energy Glossary

**Quick reference for solar terminology, units, and acronyms used throughout Denowatts documentation.**

Reference this glossary when reading feature docs. Solar terms are linked inline (e.g., "{{EPI}}").

---

## Energy & Power Units

### kW (Kilowatt)
**Power (instantaneous).** How much electrical power the system is producing *right now*, in thousands of watts. 
- 1 kW = 1,000 watts
- Example: A site with "100 kW capacity" can produce up to 100,000 watts simultaneously on a sunny day.

### kWh (Kilowatt-hour)
**Energy (cumulative over time).** How much electrical energy was produced, in kilowatt-hours.
- 1 kWh = power of 1 kW running for 1 hour
- Example: A site that produces 50 kW for 2 hours generates 100 kWh (50 kW × 2 hours).
- Used to bill customers and measure production.

### AC (Alternating Current)
Electrical current that alternates direction. Used by homes and grids.
- **AC Nameplate / AC Capacity:** The inverter's maximum output power in kW. A 100 kW inverter cannot output more than 100 kW.

### DC (Direct Current)
Electrical current in one direction. Generated by PV modules.
- **DC Capacity / Nameplate DC:** The total capacity of PV modules in kW. A site with 120 kW of modules produces DC power, which is inverted to AC by the inverter.
- Ratio: Sites often have more DC than AC (e.g., 120 kW DC → 100 kW AC) because inverters have losses and inefficiencies.

## Solar Performance Metrics

### EPI (Energy Performance Index)
**The ratio of actual energy produced to what was expected.** How well a solar site is actually performing compared to design predictions.

**Calculation:**
- EPI = (Actual Energy Produced) / (Expected Energy)
- Expressed as a percentage.
- Example: EPI = 95% means "the site produced 95% of what we predicted."

**Why it matters:**
- **Performance tracking:** Did the site perform as designed? Better? Worse?
- **Problem detection:** Unexpected drops in EPI indicate failures:
  - Shading (tree growth, building construction blocking sun)
  - Equipment failure (inverter malfunction, module degradation, string failure)
  - Maintenance issue (dirty modules, loose connections)
  - Weather anomaly (unusual cloud cover that wasn't in the forecast)
- **Revenue visibility:** Lower EPI = lower output = less money generated = customer unhappy.

**How to interpret:**
- **EPI > 100%:** Site is overperforming. Rare but possible (better-than-expected weather, superior installation, conservative design baseline).
- **EPI = 95–100%:** Excellent. Normal performance with minor losses.
- **EPI = 85–95%:** Good. Acceptable performance with expected losses (soiling, temperature derating, mismatch losses).
- **EPI < 85%:** **Problem.** Site is underperforming. Investigate causes:
  - Run diagnostics (check shading, equipment status, weather data).
  - Send technician to site if electronic failure suspected.
  - Clean panels if soiling is the culprit.
- **EPI trending down:** Even if currently >85%, a declining trend is a red flag (equipment aging, shading increasing, etc.).

**In-Service EPI** — EPI calculated only during daylight hours when the site is actively producing (excluding nights and extended cloudy periods).
- More accurate for performance assessment than 24-hour EPI, since it removes the "zero production" hours.

### BEPI (Benchmark EPI)
**The expected or "baseline" EPI.** The threshold below which performance is considered problematic.

**Meaning:**
- BEPI is the minimum acceptable EPI for a given site based on its design, location, and typical losses.
- Typically **85–95%** depending on:
  - Geographic location (sunny regions can achieve 95%; cloudy regions 85%)
  - Installation type (rooftop vs ground-mounted; different losses)
  - Technology (monocrystalline vs polycrystalline modules; crystalline vs thin-film)
  - Design margin (conservative vs aggressive baseline)

**How Denowatts uses it:**
- Alerts fire when actual EPI drops below BEPI (e.g., "Site EPI is 82%, below BEPI of 88%").
- Helps managers distinguish between "normal seasonal variation" and "something is broken."

**Example:**
- Site design BEPI = 88% (based on location and design)
- Last 7 days actual EPI = 91% (performing above minimum) → ✅ All good.
- Last 7 days actual EPI = 82% (below BEPI of 88%) → 🚨 Alert: Investigate underperformance.

### kWh/m² (Kilowatt-hours per square meter)
**Solar irradiance energy.** How much solar energy hits each square meter of earth per day.
- Used to normalize site comparisons across different geographic locations.
- Sunnier regions have higher kWh/m² values.

## Solar System Components

### PV Module (Photovoltaic Module)
A solar panel. Contains solar cells that convert sunlight to electricity.
- Rated in Watts (W) or kilowatts (kW).
- A 400W module produces 400 watts under standard test conditions (STC).
- Common models: 400W, 450W, 500W modules.

### Inverter
Converts DC power (from PV modules) to AC power (for homes/grids).
- Rated in kW. A 100 kW inverter handles up to 100 kW output.
- Multiple inverters may be used on large sites.

### Block (Physical Configuration Unit)
A **physical grouping of PV modules managed by one or more inverters.** The smallest unit of solar hardware configuration.

**What it is:**
- A block = a set of PV modules (strings) connected to one or more inverters
- A large site (e.g., 1 MW) might have 3–4 blocks of ~250 kW each, distributed across the property
- Each block can face a different direction (tilt and azimuth), use different module models, or be on a different building

**Examples:**
- Residential rooftop: 1 block = all modules on the south-facing roof + 1 inverter
- Small commercial: 2 blocks = 50 kW on the main building roof + 50 kW on the parking shade structure
- Utility-scale: 10 blocks = distributed across a large field, each with its own inverter string

**Why blocks matter:**
- **Separate monitoring:** Each block's performance is tracked independently, so you can see if one block underperforms (shading, failure) while others are fine.
- **Different characteristics:** Blocks can have:
  - Different tilt angles (south-facing vs east-facing)
  - Different azimuth (rotation direction; affects morning vs afternoon output)
  - Different module types (some newer, some older)
  - Different inverter brands or ratings
  - Different weather exposure (one block shaded at 2 PM; another not)
- **Diagnostics:** If block A produces 300 kW but block B produces 280 kW on an identical day, block B has a problem (soiling, equipment failure, shading).

**In the system:**
- Blocks are editable in the Site General Info page
- Changes to a block's specs (tilt, azimuth, module count) affect the energy model predictions
- Block-level data flows into analytics and alerts

### Tracker / Tracking System
A mechanical system that rotates PV modules to follow the sun throughout the day.
- Increases energy production by 20–35% vs fixed installations.
- Types: Single-axis (rotates E-W), Dual-axis (rotates E-W and N-S).

### Gateway / DENO Gateway
A hardware device that collects data from PV modules, inverters, and sensors.
- Sends data to Denowatts cloud via internet (WiFi, cellular, Ethernet).
- DENO = Denowatts Edge Node Orchestrator (the specific gateway model).
- Communicates via Modbus or proprietary protocols.

### Modbus
**Communication protocol** used by inverters, gateways, and sensors to exchange data.
- Modbus RTU: Serial communication (e.g., RS-485)
- Modbus TCP: Over IP network (Ethernet, WiFi)
- Denowatts gateways collect Modbus data from inverters every few minutes.

## Solar Site Characteristics

### Service Status
The operational state of a solar installation through its lifecycle:

**QUOTED** — Proposal stage only
- A solar installation has been designed and proposed to the customer, but not yet ordered.
- Timeline: Varies. Some quotes are active for months; old quotes (>6 months) are typically dead deals.
- Portfolio impact: Counts towards revenue pipeline. High QUOTED count = strong sales pipeline; stale QUOTED = dead leads.

**ORDERED** — Order placed, equipment in transit
- The customer has accepted the proposal. Equipment (PV modules, inverters, mounting hardware, gateway) has been ordered from manufacturers and is in the supply chain.
- Timeline: Typically 4–12 weeks, depending on lead times and shipping.
- Portfolio impact: Tracks committed revenue. Long wait = supply chain delays or demand surge.

**SHIPPED** — Equipment delivered, awaiting installation
- Physical equipment has arrived on-site. Installation hasn't begun yet. The gateway (data collection device) may be on-site but not yet configured.
- Timeline: Days to weeks. Long durations indicate scheduling conflicts or field team capacity issues.
- Portfolio impact: System is close to going live. Short status duration is healthy; long duration = delays.

**COMMISSIONING** — Installation in progress, testing
- Active installation and testing phase. Crews are mounting modules, wiring inverters, configuring the gateway, and running electrical tests to verify safety and performance.
- Timeline: 2–8 weeks, depending on complexity (single-phase vs three-phase, residential vs utility-scale, rooftop vs ground-mounted).
- Portfolio impact: **Highest-risk phase**. Equipment can be damaged, configurations can be wrong, crews can be delayed. Close monitoring essential.
- Key milestone: First power-up / grid connection when the system goes live.

**ACTIVE_AND_LEARNING** — System operating, building performance baseline
- The system is live and generating power. The software is learning how well it *should* perform under typical conditions.
- Timeline: 1–3 months. During this time, the system collects historical weather and production data to build an **energy model** (mathematical prediction of expected output).
- Portfolio impact: System is generating revenue but predictions are still uncertain. Baseline isn't locked yet.
- End of phase: When enough historical data has been collected (typically triggered manually by admin or automatically after 60 days).

**ACTIVE_NOT_LEARNING** — System operating normally, baseline locked
- The system is fully operational and the energy baseline is complete and locked. Performance is now predictable.
- Timeline: Indefinite (until decommissioning).
- Portfolio impact: Steady revenue generation. Alerts are based on deviation from locked baseline. Site should be self-sufficient.
- Key metric: **Energy Performance Index (EPI)** — if EPI drops below 85%, the site is underperforming (requires investigation).

**DISCONTINUED** — Decommissioned or offline
- The system has reached end-of-life, been uninstalled, or is permanently offline. No longer generating power or receiving monitoring.
- Portfolio impact: Not counted in active fleet. Archived for historical records and compliance.

### Connection Status
Whether the **gateway** (data collection device) is actively reporting data to Denowatts cloud:

**CONNECTED** — Data flowing normally
- The gateway is online and sending data every 15 minutes (or configured interval).
- Site productivity: Visible and trackable. Customers can see real-time production. Alerts work.
- Portfolio health: Green status on the map.

**PARTIALLY_CONNECTED** — Some channels connected, some offline
- The gateway is online but only *some* of its data streams are reporting (e.g., inverter 1 reporting, inverter 2 silent).
- Likely causes: 
  - One inverter failed or lost network connection (Modbus timeout).
  - One data channel disconnected (sensor cable loose, Modbus device unreachable).
  - Gateway Modbus port issue.
- Portfolio health: Yellow/amber status on the map. Not a complete outage but performance visibility is incomplete.
- Action needed: Diagnose which channels are offline and why. May require site visit.

**DISCONNECTED** — No data received
- The gateway hasn't reported data for >24 hours (configurable timeout).
- Likely causes:
  - Gateway lost internet connection (WiFi down, cellular modem failure, Lattigo SIM inactive, broadband outage).
  - Gateway powered off or crashed.
  - Gateway configuration corrupted.
  - Firewall or network policy blocking the gateway.
- Portfolio health: Red status on the map. Production invisible, no alerts, no revenue visibility.
- Action needed: Urgent. Check internet connectivity, restart gateway, verify cellular plan is active, investigate network logs.

### Commercial Operation Date (COD)
The date the site started generating power for the grid/customer.
- Important for billing, warranty start, and performance benchmarking.

### Capacity Test
A **controlled measurement of the solar site's actual maximum power output** under standard conditions.

**What it is:**
- A formal test where technicians:
  1. Operate the site at full power (all strings connected, all inverters enabled)
  2. Measure actual DC power (from modules) and AC power (from inverters) 
  3. Record weather conditions (temperature, irradiance, cloud cover)
  4. Plot actual power output vs weather to build a performance curve
- Takes place over several days to capture varying conditions (full sun, partial clouds, different times of day)

**Why it's important:**
- **Design verification:** Does the site produce what the design said it would?
- **Baseline calibration:** Actual measured performance becomes the baseline for future comparisons. All future EPI calculations depend on this.
- **Problem detection:** If measured power is 20% below design, something's wrong (modules undersized, inverter clipped, wiring loss, soiling).
- **Refinement:** Results feed back into the energy model. "In practice, this site produces X kW on Y irradiance" is more accurate than "design expects Z kW."

**Output:**
- **8760 hourly predictions for the year:** For each hour of the year, the system predicts "on this date/time with typical local weather, the site will produce X kWh."
- These predictions become the expected output used to calculate EPI going forward.

**Timeline:**
- Scheduled after ACTIVE_AND_LEARNING phase (1–3 months of operation).
- Actual test takes 3–10 days depending on weather cooperation.
- Results are used to "lock in" the energy baseline and transition to ACTIVE_NOT_LEARNING.

**Common issues:**
- Weather delays (cloudy days prolong testing).
- Incomplete data (if gateway drops connection during test, data is invalid).
- Pre-existing problems (if a module is already failing, test results are low and misleading).

## Irradiance & Weather

### Irradiance (Horizontal Irradiance)
**Solar power hitting the earth right now, per square meter.**
- Measured in W/m² (watts per square meter).
- Varies by time of day (0 at night, ~1000 W/m² at noon on a clear day).
- Used to predict instantaneous power output.

### Irradiation (Plane-of-Array Irradiation)
**Cumulative solar energy hitting tilted PV modules over time.**
- Measured in kWh/m² per day (or month, year).
- Accounts for module tilt and azimuth.
- More relevant to actual site performance than horizontal irradiance.

### Shade / Shading Factor
**Loss of production due to shadows on modules.**
- Caused by trees, buildings, terrain, clouds.
- Denowatts learns the shade profile from historical data to account for it in predictions.

### Bifacial Modules
PV modules with cells on both front and back.
- Front side collects direct sunlight.
- Back side collects reflected light from ground.
- Produce 15–30% more energy than single-sided modules in reflective environments.

## Denowatts-Specific Terms

### Energy Model
A **mathematical prediction of how much energy a solar site will produce** given weather conditions.

**Two phases of the energy model:**

**1. Predicted Energy Model (Design-Based)**
- Built before or during commissioning using design specifications
- Inputs: PV module nameplate power, inverter ratings, tilt angle, azimuth, location coordinates, shading analysis
- Output: "On a standard sunny day with 800 W/m² irradiance, the site will produce X kWh"
- Accuracy: **Moderate.** Design specs often don't match reality (modules underperform under heat, soiling losses, wiring losses, inverter inefficiency, weather patterns vary)
- Used during COMMISSIONING and early ACTIVE_AND_LEARNING phases
- Used to set initial BEPI expectations

**2. Learned Energy Model (Data-Based)**
- Built after 1–3 months of actual operating data collection
- Inputs: Historical weather data (irradiance, temperature, cloud cover), actual measured power output, location coordinates, block configuration
- Output: Machine learning model trained on "we saw this weather pattern and produced Y kWh" → generalizes to "future similar weather will produce similar power"
- Accuracy: **Much higher.** Based on real-world performance, accounting for actual losses, soiling, seasonal patterns, etc.
- Replaces the predicted model during ACTIVE_AND_LEARNING → ACTIVE_NOT_LEARNING transition
- Used for ongoing EPI calculations and revenue forecasting

**3. Monthly Energy Model (Seasonal Breakdown)**
- Predictions broken down by month (Jan, Feb, ..., Dec) to account for seasonal variation
- Example: "July typically has 45,000 kWh production due to long daylight and clear skies. January typically has 28,000 kWh due to short days and weather."
- More accurate for multi-year forecasts and financial planning
- Accounts for solar resource variation (summer peak, winter low in temperate climates)

**Why managers care:**
- **Revenue forecasting:** "How much energy will the site produce this year?" → Energy model predicts it → $/kWh = revenue
- **Performance comparison:** Actual vs predicted = EPI = site health
- **Anomaly detection:** If production suddenly drops, energy model flags it as "unusual compared to historical pattern"
- **Customer communication:** "You should expect ~1,200 kWh/month" (from energy model) → if actual is 900 kWh, customer complains and you investigate

### KPI (Key Performance Indicator)
Metrics tracked per site per time period (week, month, year):
- **EPI** — Energy Performance Index (actual vs expected)
- **Energy Availability** — Percent of time the site was capable of operating (excludes maintenance downtime)
- **Equipment Availability** — Percent of time equipment was functioning (excludes failed inverters, etc.)

### Virtual Site Builder
A drag-and-drop UI tool to visually design a site's physical layout and data flow.
- Users place "nodes" (gateways, sensors, inverters) and draw connections.
- The system validates that connections make sense (e.g., inverter can only connect to PV modules, not other inverters).

### Event / Alert / Alarm
Different levels of notifications:
- **Event:** Something happened (data point, status change)
- **Alert:** Noteworthy event requiring attention (low production, connection loss)
- **Alarm:** Critical issue requiring immediate action (inverter failure, grid disconnect)

### Flagged Event
An event manually marked by a technician as "needs investigation."
- Used to track issues requiring service or repairs.

### Ticket
A support request or work order.
- Open tickets track outstanding maintenance or troubleshooting tasks.

## Hardware & Configuration

### Channel
A **data stream from a single piece of solar equipment** — an inverter, a sensor, a meter, a string monitor, etc.

**Examples:**
- "Inverter A" (top-of-array 250 kW inverter) → one channel → sends AC power, voltage, frequency, temperature
- "Temperature Sensor" → one channel → sends ambient temp and module back-sheet temperature
- "DC Power Meter" → one channel → sends cumulative DC energy and instantaneous DC power
- "Grid Meter" → one channel → sends AC energy fed to grid and current
- "Denobox (gateway)" → one channel → sends gateway status, connectivity, health

**How channels work:**
1. Each piece of equipment (inverter, sensor, meter) has a communication protocol (Modbus RTU, Modbus TCP, OPC, API, proprietary)
2. The **gateway** (data collection device) communicates with each equipment piece via its protocol
3. The gateway extracts data from each channel (e.g., "Inverter A is outputting 248.5 kW right now")
4. The gateway sends all channel data to Denowatts cloud every 15 minutes (or configured interval)
5. Denowatts stores and displays the channel data

**Channel status:**
- **ACTIVE:** Channel is enabled and should be reporting data
- **INACTIVE:** Channel is disabled (e.g., a backup inverter not currently in use)
- **CONNECTED:** Channel is currently reporting data to the gateway
- **DISCONNECTED:** Channel hasn't reported data for >24 hours (equipment offline, communication failure, gateway issue)

**Why channels matter:**
- **Granular visibility:** Each channel = one piece of equipment. If Inverter A fails, only its channel goes silent; Inverter B still reports.
- **Problem diagnosis:** "Total site output is down 40%. Let's check each inverter channel." If one is silent, that's the culprit.
- **Data quality:** Channels send raw instrument data, which Denowatts uses to compute site KPIs, EPI, alarms, etc.
- **Multi-technology sites:** A site might have:
  - 2 Fronius inverters (Modbus TCP)
  - 1 SMA inverter (Modbus RTU)
  - 1 temperature sensor (4-20mA input)
  - 1 grid meter (OPC UA)
  - Each is a separate channel with its own communication protocol

**Channel configuration:**
- Done during commissioning in the Field Setup wizard
- Admin specifies which equipment is on-site, its protocol, its address/ID, and how often to poll
- Changes require admin access and are tracked as configuration events

### Remote Management Service
Ability to monitor and control gateways remotely (restart, update firmware, change settings) without on-site access.

### OPC / OPC UA (OLE for Process Control)
**Communication protocol** for industrial automation and data exchange.
- Used by some inverters and sensors (especially ABB, Siemens).
- More complex than Modbus but offers more features.

### Cellular Modem / Lattigo
A cellular device (modem) that sends data via mobile network (4G, 5G) instead of broadband internet.
- Lattigo is a third-party provider that manages SIM cards, data plans, and device state.
- Used when broadband is unavailable.

## Financial & Contractual

### Subscription Plan
A service tier determining what features the site has access to:
- **BASIC:** Core monitoring
- **ADVANCED:** Advanced analytics, forecasting
- **ENTERPRISE:** Custom features, priority support
- **ESSENTIAL_WEATHER:** Weather data integration
- **ENERGY_ACCOUNTING:** Detailed energy tracking

### Quote / Quotation
A formal proposal for a solar installation, including:
- Equipment specs (modules, inverters, capacity)
- Estimated performance (annual energy production)
- Cost and timeline
- Converted to an Order once customer accepts.

### Work Log / Service Record
Historical record of maintenance, repairs, or inspections at a site.
- Used to correlate downtime with service events.

## Data & Monitoring

### Timezone Awareness
Denowatts automatically detects and stores each site's timezone (based on GPS coordinates) to:
- Correctly timestamp data (sunrise/sunset times vary by location)
- Aggregate performance metrics across distributed sites
- Display times to local users correctly

### Polling Interval
How often Denowatts queries for new data:
- **Real-time dashboards:** 60-second polling (1-minute delay)
- **Backend aggregations:** 5–15 minute intervals (balance between freshness and load)

### Soft Delete
Data is marked as deleted but not permanently removed (deletedAt timestamp set).
- Allows recovery if deletion was accidental.
- Maintains referential integrity (linked records still valid).
